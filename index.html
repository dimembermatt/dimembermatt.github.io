EE302 Final Project

The EE302 final project was for me, many things. It was a delight, since it was the first interaction we had with anything near robotics and real-application circuits at UT ECE. It was a huge learning curve, since the last lab was basically "How to read a photoresistor." And it was a huge amount of angst, after maybe a week of building to find out that we were severely imited by hardware problems.

I and one of my teammates, Brent, were in charge with the circuit design and robot assembly and our other teammates did the programming and software in Labview. It was only until the night before during debugging that we found out that our threshold sensing from our photoresistors weren't calibrating properly at all, so our robot would vary dramatically in its ability to line follow across runs.

The hour before our test, we found out that as we held the photoresistor above the line the values decreased indefinitely! Soldering, taping, wire twisting the photoresistor lines together didn't help at all, so this was a big lesson for us in the importance of hardware, which was later compounded for me at the later stages of Region V and Pacbot.

Final Product: The Pico Bot ^2

Our EE302 Final project car, dubbed the 'Pico Bot (squared)', is one of the bulkiest entrants of its kind. In order to run its complicated line and wall following algorithms, two myDAQs are placed on a cardboard housing connected to a whole lot of sensors.

Looking from the side, the wires branch everywhere. Some even sneak in through the front hole, past the front compartment, and into the motor leads. All in all, the robot sometimes tipped over since it was too back heavy.

EE306 Labs and independent projects

Intro to Computing brought to the table a number of concepts that weren't covered in your ordinary high school CS class. The class explored the various types of instructions and their relevance -leading very smoothly into EE319K's more practical applications using the ARM Thumb-2- and at the end our final lab was to implement Connect-4 in LC-3.

EE319 Final Project

EE319K Final Project Github page

Final Product: The TivaBoy

This was, inarguably, my entire month of April this year. Ever since the Professor Yerraballi announced the final lab, all I could think about was this project.

The TivaBoy, designed using the TM4C Tiva Microcontroller, features a TFT display breakout, a 4-button D-pad, an A and B button, a volume slider, an audio jack and speaker, as well as a port for serial communication (with other TM4Cs).

It was designed so that anyone could program a game and run it on the TivaBoy (considering memory and RAM constraints). Use the volume slider to move a sprite in a bullet hell game? You can do that. A joystick using the D-pad buttons? Go right ahead. You're only limited by your imagination.

A really cool aspect of this project was that my Mech-E roommate, Jason, designed the chassis for the TivaBoy and we put it all together. One of the challenges we had was getting the D-pad to properly initiate the switches, which means it could be too loose (or it'd fall out or the user would have to press really hard in) or too tight (and the switches would always be pressed).

Wiring and soldering this project was a roller coaster ride. Hours planning where to put the components, soldering in the dorm room at two in the morning on a monday (not my best idea), and dozens of circuit QA checks to make sure nothing was broken.

Eventually, my lab partner had her awesome friend at Cirrus Labs, Augustus, help us finish soldering the other half the same day. His work was so good it brought tears to our eyes.

A week before soldering the protoboard circuit, I made this circuit diagram. It has all the features I was fantasizing about for the final lab project, and a lot (if not all) of elements in it appear in previous EE319K labs. I think it's a pretty accurate representation of all the circuit interfacing knowledge I've gained over the semester.

Link to the image on Github

As of the competition day, this is the completed product (diassembled). The game I was emulating was Pokemon, playing a Pokemon battle between the player and a rudimentary NPC. The main.c code is easily over 1k lines, not to mention all the drivers I wrote for it as well, like the virtual buffer graphics and the triple interrupts for sound. Looking back, if I had went with a simpler game and made that more amazing I would've had a better shot going into the 'superfinals' but I don't regret a thing.

IEEE RAS

2017-2018

The IEEE Robotics and Automation Society is a large organization at the University of Texas at Austin, numbering hundreds of members who actively participate in projects and community outreach across majors and colleges.

This school year, I participated as a member of the Region V, PacBot, and Robotathon committees.

Robotathon

Definitely a highlight of my freshman year, Robotathon was a fast-paced month where teams of four build autonomous robots to achieve an objective. This year the theme was "RAScars", and we had to design a robot that could maneuver an obstacle field to reach the other opponent's RAScar.

Final Product: The Pico Bot

Our Robotathon RAScar, the Picobot simple design mowed down the competition during the competition.

PicoBot's top half, waiting for glue to set. Access holes for wires and such, and the cuts on the sides for the servo motorss.

During the Robotathon Kickoff and the following workshops, we attended seminars that taught us the basics to get us started on our robot. CADding in Solidworks, writing sensor control loops using Rasware ("A generic robotics controller for the EK-LM4F120XL Stellaris Launchpad<"), even how to use Ubuntu.

In this project, I participated in the creation of the mechanical design for the PicoBot, CADding the first iteration prototype. The design in its second iteration has two layers, the second with an access hole cut to allow access to the top and bottom ports of the Launchpad, and providing more space for components such as the circuit and the sensors. The front end is angular to push obstacle blocks out of the way. Our algorithm setup was pretty simple in that we decided to use only the line sensor array in order to traverse the field. I laser cut both the prototypes (one of my favorite skills I learned this year), learning both how to operate the machines and the RetinaEngrave proprietary software.

The field we helped hastily assemble the night before competition for testing. Oh, and the tower of doom. There was an issue of the incline being too high/too low friction/too jilted for a lot of RAScars to get over, but that was settled by next morning.

2nd Place...Not 1st but not bad.

During the competition, our robot stomped the adversaries, driving through undefeated to win the finals. There were a lot of hiccups along the way, like getting stuck on field edges or losing sight of the track lines, but Picobot survived.

We lost first place in overall points (by a measly 6 points) since the 2nd place team did more challenges than us (the dratted wall sensing!), but we took home a nice trophy for commemoration.

Region V

Region V has definitely been a large part of my freshman year. Members of RAS grouped together in subcommittees, respectively the Hardware, Mechanical, and Software groups, and we were tasked with creating an autonomous robot that roamed across a field and collected metal tokens, sorting them by color and depositing them into their respective boxes.

The front final assembly of our Region V robot.

The top final assembly of our Region V robot.

I worked this year in the Mechanical subcommittee (for motivations unknown to even myself), and I participated in the design of the formulative robot. We split it down and developed further, and I was tasked with much of the assembly and creation of its respective parts.

It was in this club that I learned about and spent much of my time in the UT makerspace and woodshop, developing further skills in using Solidworks, RetinaEngrave, and Inkscape. I worked with and around the laser cutters as well as the 3d printers (CraftBot and its respective Craftware software), and got licensed to use the machine shop and its mills and lathes.

Working on this robot was a lot of fun, and although we had kinks during its development (PCB failures at the testing stage, height limitations when we found out some parts were too high, motor control issues, etc), this was a great experience in learning about how important design and iteration is during the development of robots and other products that require interaction between different fields of engineering.

Although I missed out on the competition and was knocked in bed until the afternoon of, we got 10th place out of 30 teams. And judging by the competition, that was pretty good since the top five teams were from the same school with pretty much the exact same robot.

The-mostly-constructed assembly of our Region V robot.

PacBot

PacBot was a committee that started in the second semester of my freshman year. Held by the Harvard Undergraduate Robotics Club, Pacbot is more closely similar to MicroMouse in that the autonomous robot -small enough to fit in the palm of your hand- navigates a maze. In our case, our robot was to navigate a maze and avoid ghosts, attempting to eat as many "pellets" as possible. This was achieved by using sensors to navigate the maze as well as communication with a server that displayed our position, the position of the ghosts, as well as the pellets to pick up.

First servo tests on the PacBot.

I took a role closer to my ECE major in this committee, and was involved in circuit assembly and interfacing of the Pacbot. In particular I worked with the I2C protocol and communicating between the main system with the VL6180X time of flight sensor.

Our PacBot, compared to the size of a quarter.

Testing the PacBot on the floor and seeing it drift. communication to the servos was serial, so there's a time lag between stopping one servo and another.

Our PacBot third place trophy!

Probably the biggest hurdle in the development of the PacBot was the components we were using, since one TOF was dead on arrival and we had issues trying to communicate data across I2C with a chip that didn't accept register values larger than a couple bits (with the register we wanted to access being maybe in the upper 200s). This problem persisted pretty late into the game up until the competition.

ASME Rube Goldberg

2017-2018

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers Rube Goldberg society is a subset organization where a group of people build a machine out of random, everyday parts to achieve a simple task. Rube uses this machine, and other machines built during the school year, to perform outreach to students in the Austin area and sends a final machine to the Rube Goldberg National competition.

Rube Goldberg was my break from the constant circuits and electronics. We built all sorts of stuff here, from nerd box dominos to pencil propulsed ballistas.

The gang setting up a test run for the people at Reliant.

Building the first prototype of the Rube Machine. This was presented at the Cockrell School Con outreach event.

Us presenting our machine at Cockrell School Con!

The task this year was to pour a bowl of cereal, so we designed a 20+ step machine centering around the theme "Crazy Hotel".
We had three floors, and separate rooms such as the lobby, hotel room, garden, etc, where we implemented objects such as slides, elevators, and many flying objects.

We were commissioned by Reliant Energy to help make a Rube Goldberg machine for their advertisement. It was fun and enlightening to see them film the commercial, as well as design and set up our part.

In addition to that, we also built machines for Cockrell Con and Reliant for an advertisement commercial. It was a lot of fun demonstrating to both kids and adults some of our steps, and a very fulfilling experience.

Testing the initial runs of a section of the Rube Goldberg machine for Reliant in the mechanical engineering building Rube room.

The final Reliant commercial! It's well put together, and you can see our part of the Rube machine going down the hallway and down the stairs, with the big cup pyramid.
