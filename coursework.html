Fall 2017

Intro to Electrical Engineering

This class constitutes the foundational circuit analysis techniques and knowledge required for future courses such as EE411, Circuit Theory and upper division courses in Electrical Engineering. Among the topics learned in this class are:

  • Operational Amplifiers
  • Mesh/Node Analysis
  • Thevenin and Norton Equivalents
  • Power Transfer

Final Lab: Build an autonomous car that can navigate an obstacle course.

While this lab was a culmination of all the technical skills we've learned in the course, such as soldering, interfacing, and building programs with Labview, ultimately it was a greater lesson in what not to do when designing and implementing a project such as an autonomous car.

Choose your hardware appropiately.

As is made quite evident in the picture above, our car was a splendid mishmash of trailing wires and a semi-stable cardboard frame sagging under the weight of not one, but two myDAQs plus a breadboard.

This made our car unwieldy, liable to fall over backwards at a bad touch, and very hard to debug and construct. A friendly reminder that myDAQ stands for Data AcQuisition Device, not a microcontroller.

An alternative solution to the problem was to use an Arduino microcontroller, unfortunately something we were made aware of almost the night before the lab was to be checked out. That would've massively simplified the work that would have to be done programming the control loops, as well as reduce the overall size of the car.

Another issue (and arguably the one that killed the car) was the sensors we were given -a set of photoresistors for the line sensing. If held up to the black line, with and without direct illumination, the output voltage would decrease over time without fail. This means that our efforts to threshold the line through software and hardware ended up with little fruit. If I were to go back in time, I would have at the very least revised the development timeline to have first tested the sensors and changed them out before integrating them and then testing them.

This case is a pretty obvious application of the lesson; although it has popped up outside of class, particularly in my experiences with IEEE RAS Region V's 2017 robot and constructing testing setups for UTSVT's solar array. The proper research and use of equipment, even as trivial as the connectors between solar cells, play a pivotal role in the development of any project.

Intro to Computing

Intro to Computing is a bottom-up approach of understanding the complexity of computers, from low (low) level bitgates to the LC3-A instruction set architecture and assembly. This is the foundational class to EE319K, Intro to Embedded Systems, EE460N, Computer Architecture, and many more classes.

The final lab for this class consists of creating a fully operational game in LC3 Assembly, in this case Connect Four. Students were expected to manage I/O, create subroutines, and maintain memory of the state of the game until an end condition is reached.

A particularly interesting problem I encountered during this lab was that my program became large enough that PC-relative assembler instructions were unable to retrieve data from where they were stored. My solution for this was to create a subroutine that imported the "global variables" into local variables closer to the methods in order to provide a read-only copy. Since it was only read only, this subroutine is good only for functions such as checking for four in a row.

Another program that I made after the last lab ended was an LC3 program based off of the game of war. I was interested in learning about pseudorandom number generators, and wanted to create my own. I initially used a friend's prng he made for his LC3 implementation of Conway's Game of Life, and used that to make a primitive Game of War card game that unfortunately lacked a deck feature, so it only gave the player and the CPU a pseudorandom card based on an input seed.

However, I then created my own PRNG using the Linear Congruential Method referenced from Donald Knuth, the author of The Art of Computer Programming. In the future EE312 Intro to Software Design class, I and my partner will implement Go Fish in C++, that works with a pregenerated deck.

Spring 2018

Intro to Embedded Systems

Easily my favorite class of the semester, Intro to Embedded Systems explores the interaction between computers and the outside world. Among the topics covered during this class are:

  • interfacing
  • analog to digital converters and vice versa
  • communication with SPI
  • interrupts

In this class I worked with the Tiva TM4C microcontroller, using at first ARM Thumb2 Assembly to create things like a finite state machine traffic light and then later on, C to make a keyboard with switches. Our final project involved creating a whole game from scratch, and I created and soldered the interfacing for the TivaBoy.

One of the really taxing parts of this project was implementing the game itself, a stripped down version of a Pokemon battle. I spent a lot of time getting the fundamental TivaBoy working, drivers and all, and allocated too little time (context: this was my entire month of May) to actually program the game. In retrospect, it would have been less stressful to implement a mechanically simpler game, like Pong, and add an interesting twist on it. In addition, it would probably be better, too.

However, I'm pretty glad overall about this project, since it allowed me to encounter a lot of the problems that a developer would face when working to integrate their application with the base hardware, such as creating and manipulating graphics and sound. I did come up with a couple cool solutions, such as a virtual buffer and repurposing the previous piano lab to output music with the pitiful memory requirements we had.

Credits to my lab partner, Tatiana Flores, who helped me throughout our labs to allow me to learn so much, my roommate, Jason Nguyen, for CADding and 3d printing the chassis, and Augustus, who lended his solder skills to beautifully finish the first TivaBoy.

The TivaBoy is still an ongoing project, and I've designed it so that its modular and any game I can think of can probably be programmed on there. I'm currently redesigning a plan in which I can play more advanced music (see chords) with the DAC using a custom file format that is generated with OpenCV using optical music recognition.

How Things Work

How Thinks Work is a class that explores the design of processes and objects, from traffic simulations to cars. This class invites a lot of outside professors and industry professionals to come and talk about what they do, and how does what they do work. Among the many interesting talks held in the class is one about the Innovation Station, a 3D printing vending machine created by UT mechanical engineering students in 2014.

This class focuses in on the design process, from ideation to customer feedback, and from prototyping to refinement. Each discussion session decides on a singular type of product to make, and groups go out and make their own prototypes of that that product. For our group, we decided to make an automated pet feeder to tackle the issue of leaving a pet at home alone for a vacation, or reducing the workload of what was previously a tedious chore.

In this project, I led the development of the circuitry and interfacing of sensors to the pet feeder, as well as the arduino programing and testing to integrate all the sensors together. The electrical components consisted of three potentiometers that adjusted the time of feeding and stepper motor that adjusted the opening to allow food to come out of the nozzle of the pet feeder.

Altogether, it was a pretty simple project programming wise, but one of the issues we had was putting all of the wires and the microcontroller into the control box. The end solution was to carefully align the wiring so that there was minimal width used along the breadboard to properly place it between the Arduino and the knob potentiometers.

Another issue design wise that we stumbled upon was the slider that allowed food in and out. There is a little groove that keeps the slider in place, but that can jam up with pet food. A solution to this would be to remove the lower groove and keep the top groove to keep the slider, or even better, modify the slider be raised vertically instead of horizontally.

Overall, this class taught me a lot about design requirements and the design process, especially the need to iteratively design in order to probe out problems in earlier prototypes. I think this skill is definitely important when working for a company that creates a physical (or even virtual) product in order to create a polished idea.

Fall 2018

Software Design and Implementation

This class builds upon the C learned in EE319K, Intro to Embedded Systems, as well as going into C++ in the latter half of the course. Topics taught in this class includes:

  • heap memory allocation (malloc, free, new)
  • object oriented programming
  • recursion
  • intro to Big O notation
  • data structures such as linked lists, stacks, vectors, trees, heaps, hashmaps
  • sorting implementations (i.e. mergesort, quicksort, etc)

While a lot of this class (particularly the first half of it) was review for me, who learned Java and OOP in high school, much more of this class was an interesting delve into data structures and algorithms that I was previously unaware of, including hashmaps and priority queues using heaps.

A majority of this class was also applied learning and practice. Among the many labs during this semester were making a game of Go Fish, building a fake IPod, and using hashmaps to find plagiarism among file sets. The last one was particularly fun, and my partner and I were quite enthused in reducing the run time of a >1.5million word set of files into sub 2.5 seconds while maintaining good accuracy and precision.

Among the cool algorithms that I worked on was a recursive floodfill, and while it was pretty simple, I had a lot of fun drawing it out and building it.

Development of a Solar Powered Vehicle

This class takes the theoretical knowledge from Circuit Theory and Software Development and puts it into the real, practical work of building a solar vehicle for the 2019 Formula Sun Grand Prix. In class, we cover topics from all disciplines, from nuclear fusion to solar energy conversion to aerodynamics. Outside of class, we meet up at the Pickle campus to participate in one of the major subgroups, such as body, communications, or suspension.

I am involved with the solar array group, and our primary focus is to fabricate solar modules for application onto our 2019 car, BeVolt. On thursdays, we meet up in the lab in the EER to test cells, and oftentimes, I come in early to solder up a module.

At Pickle, I finish up the modules while the rest of the team sets up the lamination bed, and we concurrently laminate modules while prepping new ones. The process is often long and fraught with danger, since the slightest touch or mistake can irreparably break a cell or an entire module.

But our group gets our work done with great care and efficiency, and over the semester, we have ramped up our speed from a single 1v1 test cell to three 4x2 modules in a single day. For me, what might have previously taken two hours to solder a 4x2 module together now only takes about 20 to 40 minutes.

I've learned a great deal thanks to this class, and have become the Solar Array Lead for the 2019 year. I look forward to working with UTSVT to finish our car for competition this summer and winning the race!
